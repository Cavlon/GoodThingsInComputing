Smart Bandages Developed by NUS Scientists Show How Computer Science is Enhancing Healthcare

A team at the National University of Singapore has created a smart bandage that uses a range of sensors to monitor injuries.

Photo by cottonbro

What do these smart bandages do?

The smart bandages developed by the team at NUS provide remote, real-time assessments of a patient's wound, which are transmitted wirelessly to a mobile app. To facilitate these assessments, the bandage can sense a range of important characteristics of a wound, from temperature and pH to the type of bacteria present.

How do they work?

Over the course of a 15 minute test, the bandage uses its sensors to record data such as temperature and pH, but also provides more insight into the infection through information the sensor gathers from the fluid present at the wound. Then, the integrated chip wirelessly transmits the data recorded to a mobile device throught the "VeCare" application.

Why are they useful?

For chronic wounds, ensuring treatment is being administered often enough to prevent serious issues and promote healing can mean several lengthy check-ups with doctors, which means patients may have to make many trips to hospital to get the appropriate care. By using the smart bandage, patients can monitor the status of the wound themselves, without the need to see a doctor in person. It also allows for more regular readings of the wound's status.
